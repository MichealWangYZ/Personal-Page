Projects

NV Center Project

Simultaneous determination of local magnetic fields and sensor orientation with Nitrogen-Vacancy centers in nanodiamond

This project focuses on a significant challenge: simultaneously determining both the orientation of a sensor and the local magnetic field. The initial approach involved deriving an analytical model to map applied bias fields to magnetic splittings. However, this proved to be complex. Through a process of physically motivated refinement, we transitioned from equation-solving to a more robust optimization-based approach. We realized that a naive optimization would be computationally expensive due to the large number of parameters. Our key insight was to disentangle the problem into two sequential steps: first, determining the magnetic field, and second, determining the sensor orientation. By transforming the multi-degree-of-freedom problem into a simpler trigonometric one, we were able to prove that a minimum of four distinct bias magnetic fields are sufficient to determine the local magnetic field accurately.

CsYbSe2 Project

Pressure-induced Crystal Structural and Insulator-Metal Transitions in the Quantum Spin Liquid Candidate CsYbSe2

This was a large-scale computational project involving a system with ten f-electrons, which is typically computationally prohibitive. We used physical intuition to dramatically simplify the problem. By analyzing the chemical environment, we hypothesized that nine of the 4f electrons were "frozen" and not participating in bonding. This contradicted standard DFT-level calculations which suggested the material was a conductor. By applying a physics-inspired method and using a custom Ytterbium pseudo-potential with the f-electrons frozen, we were able to dramatically reduce the computational cost while providing an accurate prediction for the insulating phase, which aligned well with experimental results. This demonstrates the power of using physical insight to overcome computational bottlenecks.

Twist-Driven Exciton Funnel Effect in Bilayer Boron Nitride

Twist-Driven Deep-Ultraviolet-Wavelength Exciton Funnel Effect in Bilayer Boron Nitride

My contribution to this project was using molecular dynamics (MD) to simplify complex calculations. Previously, simulating twisted systems required calculating the interactions of tens of thousands of atoms, an incredibly expensive process. I developed a method that only requires knowing the local stacking style, band structure, and how the twisting distorts the atomic forces, which is efficiently captured by LAMMPS. I quantified two types of strain: a pressure-like effect (total length compression) and a dragging effect (deviation of a central atom from its neighbors). This approach, leveraging the computational efficiency of MD ($O(N)$ vs. $O(N^2)$ or higher for DFT/GW), dramatically simplified the calculation.

Kicked Rotor Project

Dramatic Delocalization induced by mutually interactive kicked rotor

This project explores the fascinating phenomenon of two mutually interacting components. We found that coupling two insulators can lead to a conductor-like state. This time-series problem was analyzed by first proposing a tight-binding model, which was further optimized using a Fourier transform to reduce computational costs. We then investigated the underlying mechanisms, performing both analytical and numerical studies to determine that the delocalization is primarily driven by a scattering-like behavior rather than a phase-induced effect.

Undergraduate Thesis: Resonant Atomic Arrays

Optimization of coherent scattering of resonate atomic arrays

My undergraduate thesis focused on an optimization problem: arranging a small number of atoms to maximize light scattering from a Gaussian beam. This was a complex many-body problem with a large number of independent parameters. To reduce the computational cost, I used physics intuition to identify a more limited, but physically meaningful, set of possible atomic arrangements. By focusing on these patterns, I was able to dramatically reduce the solution space and the number of optimization parameters, leading to a much more efficient and successful optimization process.

BEC-BCS Crossover Project

Self-consistent calculation of the spectral function of BEC-BCS Crossover State using Pade Method

In this project, I performed a one-shot Green's function calculation to analyze the spectral function of an ultra-cold fermionic condensate. I used the Pade method for fitting, which is advantageous for capturing the correct asymptotic behavior of a Green's function. During this process, I discovered a numerical instability with the standard `numpy.linalg` library in the high-frequency region. I resolved this by switching to a more robust `scipy` method and implementing a weighted fitting approach. By giving more weight to the high-frequency points, I was able to ensure better convergence and preserve the correct high-frequency decay behavior, providing a more accurate spectral function.
